Help & Concepts
How to use this simulator
  1. Select a circuit type from the left panel
  2. Enter the component values (Rin, Rf, Vin, Vs)
  3. Select a waveform type (Sine, Square, Triangle)
  4. Select an op-amp model — Ideal or a real chip
  5. If using a real chip, enter the input frequency
  6. Click Calculate to see results and waveform
  7. Change the op-amp model to see how non-idealities affect the output
Circuit types
Inverting Amplifier
Amplifies the input signal and inverts it (180° phase shift). Gain = −Rf/Rin. Most common op-amp configuration used in signal processing and audio.
Non-inverting Amplifier
Amplifies without inverting. Gain = 1 + Rf/Rin. Always has gain ≥ 1. Used when phase must be preserved.
Voltage Follower
Output equals input (gain = 1). Used as a buffer to connect a high impedance source to a low impedance load without signal loss.
Summing Amplifier
Adds multiple input signals together. Each input has its own resistor. Vout = −Rf(V1/R1 + V2/R2 + ...). Used in audio mixers and DACs.
Integrator
Output is the integral of the input. Uses a capacitor as feedback. Converts square waves to triangles, sine waves to cosines. Gain decreases with frequency.
Differentiator
Output is the derivative of the input. Capacitor at input, resistor as feedback. Converts triangles to squares. Gain increases with frequency — sensitive to noise.
Non-idealities explained
Offset Voltage (Vos)
Even with 0V input, a real op-amp produces a small DC error at the output due to mismatched internal transistors. This error is amplified by the circuit gain. LM741 has 2mV offset — at gain 100 this becomes 200mV error.
Slew Rate
The maximum speed at which the output can change (V/μs). If the input demands faster change than this limit, the output distorts — sine waves become triangular, square wave edges become ramps. LM741: 0.5V/μs. OPA2134: 20V/μs.
Gain-Bandwidth Product (GBW)
Gain × Bandwidth = constant. At gain=10, LM741 only works up to 100kHz. At gain=100, only 10kHz. Higher gain = less usable bandwidth. OPA2134 has 8MHz GBW vs LM741's 1MHz.
Input Bias Current
A tiny current that flows into the op-amp inputs. Flowing through feedback resistors, it creates an additional voltage error. JFET-input op-amps (TL071, OPA2134) have much lower bias current than BJT-input (LM741, LM358).
CMRR / PSRR
CMRR (Common Mode Rejection Ratio) measures how well the op-amp rejects noise common to both inputs. PSRR measures rejection of power supply noise. Higher values (dB) = better noise rejection.

How to use Design Mode
  1. Click Switch to Design Mode in the top bar
  2. Select the circuit type you want to design
  3. Enter your target gain — what gain do you need?
  4. Enter a starting Rin — 10kΩ is a good default
  5. Enter the required bandwidth — highest frequency your signal will have
  6. Enter the input amplitude — peak voltage of your input signal
  7. Enter your supply voltage
  8. Set max gain error % — how much deviation from target is acceptable
  9. Click Find Components to see recommended resistors and chip verification
What does chip verification mean?
The simulator checks every op-amp chip against your specifications:

✓ Pass — chip meets all your specs. Safe to use.
⚠ Warning — chip works but is near its limits. Use with caution.
✗ Fail — chip cannot meet your specs. Choose a different one.
What is E12 resistor series?
Real resistors only come in standard values: 10, 12, 15, 18, 22, 27, 33, 39, 47, 56, 68, 82 kΩ (and multiples). The simulator calculates the exact Rf you need, then finds the nearest available standard value and shows you the resulting gain error.
Why does bandwidth matter for chip selection?
Every op-amp has a fixed Gain-Bandwidth Product (GBW). At higher gains, the usable bandwidth drops. For example LM741 with gain=10 only works up to 100kHz. If your signal goes higher, the output will be attenuated and distorted.
